Planning Inclusive Events

Make your events welcoming and accessible to all attendees, whether in-person, virtual, or hybrid.

Reading time: 10 minutes

Why Inclusive Events Matter

  • 1 in 4 adultsin the U.S. has a disability
  • Accessible events benefit everyone (parents with strollers, people with temporary injuries, etc.)
  • Required by law (ADA) for public events at public universities
  • Creates better experiences and increases attendance

Planning Phase

Ask About Accommodations Early

Include this in every event registration:

"If you require any accommodations to participate in this event, please contact [name] at [email] at least [X days] in advance."
  • Ask early (at least 2 weeks before for simple accommodations, more for complex needs)
  • Follow up with requesters to understand their needs
  • Keep accommodation requests confidential

Budget for Accessibility

Build these costs into your event budget:

  • Sign language interpreters
  • CART (real-time captioning)
  • Accessible venue features
  • Alternative format materials
  • Assistive listening devices

Form an Inclusive Planning Team

  • Include people with disabilities in planning
  • Assign someone as accessibility coordinator
  • Consult with the Disability Resource Center for complex events

Venue Selection (In-Person)

Physical Accessibility

Seating Arrangements

  • Reserve front-row seating for people who lip-read or use interpreters
  • Leave space for wheelchair users to sit with companions
  • Ensure sightlines aren't blocked
  • Provide a variety of seating options (chairs with arms, without arms)

Signage & Wayfinding

  • Clear, high-contrast signs
  • Large text (minimum 1" tall for distance viewing)
  • Signs at consistent heights
  • Braille signage for rooms

Virtual Events

Platform Selection

Choose accessible platforms like:

  • Zoom— Good keyboard accessibility, live captions, screen reader support
  • Microsoft Teams— Built-in live captions, good accessibility
  • Webex— Accessible with screen readers

Test with assistive technology before the event.

Virtual Event Checklist

Zoom Accessibility Settings

  1. EnableLive Transcriptionin account settings
  2. Turn on captions before sharing the invite link
  3. Allow participants to pin/spotlight interpreters
  4. EnableGallery Viewso users can see interpreters

Accessible Presentations

Slide Design

  • Use high-contrast colors (dark text on light background)
  • Minimum 24pt font for body text, 36pt for titles
  • Sans-serif fonts (Arial, Calibri, Verdana)
  • Limit text per slide (6 lines max)
  • Avoid animations that flash or auto-advance

Presenting Tips

  • Describe visuals:"This graph shows enrollment increasing from 1000 to 5000 over 5 years"
  • Pace yourself:Pause to allow interpreters and captioners to catch up
  • Face the audience:Don't talk to the screen—people need to see your face
  • Use the microphone:Even if you think you're loud enough
  • Repeat questions:"The question was..." before answering

Materials

  • Share slides in advance (at least 24 hours)
  • Provide accessible formats (tagged PDF, Word, accessible PowerPoint)
  • Offer large print handouts on request
  • Make materials available electronically

Common Accommodations

Accommodation Who Benefits Lead Time
Sign Language Interpreters Deaf attendees 2-4 weeks
CART (Real-time Captioning) Deaf, hard-of-hearing, non-native speakers 1-2 weeks
Assistive Listening Devices Hard-of-hearing attendees Check venue availability
Reserved Seating Wheelchair users, people who lip-read Day of event
Large Print Materials Low vision attendees 1 week
Electronic Formats Blind, low vision attendees 1 week

Day-of-Event Tips

  • Have staff/volunteers trained in accessibility
  • Identify yourself and your role when speaking
  • Point interpreters to the speaker/screen as needed
  • Ensure pathways remain clear throughout
  • Have backup plans if technology fails
  • Check that live captions are working
  • Be ready to help without being asked

After the Event

  • Share recording with accurate captions
  • Provide slides and materials to attendees
  • Send follow-up survey that includes accessibility questions
  • Document what worked and what didn't for future events
