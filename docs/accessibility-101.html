Accessibility 101

Plain-language overview of what accessibility means at the University of Arizona, why it matters, and how to get help.

Updated for the Title II Web & Mobile Rule (2026) · Updated: 2025-11-20 · Next review: 2026-02-20

What we mean by accessibility

Accessibility means people can get what they need from UA—whether they use a screen reader, magnifier, captions, voice input, a keyboard, or just a cracked phone screen on bad Wi‑Fi.

It covers websites, apps, documents, media, classroom technology, events, and in-person services.

Why it matters at UA

  • Legal requirements: Title II of the ADA and Section 504/508 require public universities to provide equal access.
  • Arizona values: Inclusive design supports student success, research, recruitment, and community partnerships.
  • Everyday usability: The same changes that help disabled people also help people on phones, in noisy spaces, or with temporary injuries.

Myth vs. reality: Accessibility is not “extra work for a few users.” It is how we make sure more people can finish a task the first time.

What “good” looks like at a glance

Every team starts from a different place. Use these levels to talk honestly about where you are and what to do next.

  • Minimum: New content avoids obvious barriers such as missing alt text, unreadable contrast, uncaptioned required videos, or inaccessible PDFs for required materials.
  • Better: Your team uses checklists and templates, runs basic accessibility checks before publishing, and responds to reported barriers within a few days.
  • Best: Accessibility is built into planning, design, purchasing, and development. You test with disabled users, log decisions, and review key systems on a regular cadence.

This site is designed to help you move from minimum toward better and best without needing to be an expert before you start.

How UA teams can get help

  1. Pick your persona guide for tailored tasks, tools, and training.
  2. Use the support page or Accessibility Wizard to request reviews, captioning, procurement help, or accommodations.
  3. Report digital or physical barriers via the DRC Access Report; we aim to respond within two business days.
  4. Join trainings & office hours to build skills and share best practices.

How much time does this take? Most improvements start as 5–10 minute tweaks to things you already do—headings, links, color choices—rather than brand‑new projects.

Next steps by role: Instructors can start at the Teaching & Learning hub, staff purchasing tools can use the Procurement hub, and students or families can open the Student guide.
