Guiding Principles

The core principle is simple:treat people with disabilities with the same respect and consideration you give everyone else. These guidelines help you put that principle into practice.

Person-First vs. Identity-First Language

Some people prefer "person with a disability" (person-first), while others prefer "disabled person" (identity-first). When in doubt, follow the individual's lead or ask their preference. Both approaches are valid—what matters is respect.

General Communication Guidelines

  • Ask before providing assistance.Don't assume someone needs help—ask first and respect their answer.
  • Talk directly to the person, not through their companion, interpreter, or aide.
  • Refer to a person's disability only if relevantto the conversation.
  • Avoid derogatory language.For example, say "a person who uses a wheelchair" rather than "a person confined to a wheelchair." A wheelchair is liberating, not confining.
  • Provide information in multiple formatswhen possible (written, spoken, visual).
  • Don't interact with service animalswithout permission. They're working.
  • Don't be afraid of common phraseslike "see you later" or "let's walk through this"—they're fine.
  • Don't touch mobility devices or assistive technologywithout consent. These are extensions of personal space.
  • Don't assume physical contact is okay.Handshakes, high-fives, and hugs should be offered, not assumed.
  • Understand that not everyone uses eye contact, especially some neurodivergent individuals.

Blind or Low Vision

Do

  • Identify yourself when you approach: "Hi, this is Alex from IT."
  • Be descriptive with directions: "The coffee is about three feet to your left."
  • Describe visual information verbally during presentations.
  • Read aloud any text you're showing or distributing.
  • When guiding, offer your elbow rather than grabbing their arm.
  • Tell the person when you're leaving the conversation.
  • Describe the content of charts, graphs, and images.

Don't

  • Say "over there" or point without verbal description.
  • Grab or push the person to guide them.
  • Pet or distract a guide dog.
  • Move items without telling them.
  • Assume they can't see anything—"low vision" means some usable vision.
  • Leave without saying goodbye.

Deaf or Hard of Hearing

Do

  • Face the person when speaking so they can see your lips.
  • Speak clearly at a normal volume. Speak louder only if requested.
  • Repeat questions from audience members before answering.
  • Use paper/pencil or type on your phone if needed for clarity.
  • When using an interpreter, speak directly to the deaf person, not the interpreter.
  • Look at the person who is deaf, even when the interpreter is voicing.
  • Get the person's attention with a gentle wave or tap on the shoulder.

Don't

  • Cover your mouth while speaking.
  • Chew gum or eat while talking.
  • Turn away mid-sentence.
  • Shout (it distorts lip movements).
  • Assume all deaf people can lip-read or sign.
  • Talk to the interpreter instead of the deaf person.

Mobility Impairments

Do

  • Sit down for long conversations with wheelchair users to maintain eye level.
  • Ensure meeting spaces are physically accessible.
  • Respect personal space—the wheelchair is part of it.
  • Ask before moving items that may be needed for mobility.
  • Offer to carry items if it seems helpful, but wait for acceptance.

Don't

  • Lean on or touch someone's wheelchair.
  • Push the wheelchair without asking.
  • Move mobility aids out of reach.
  • Assume the person can't participate in activities.
  • Talk to a companion instead of the person.

Speech Impairments

Do

  • Listen carefully and patiently.
  • Repeat what you think you understood, then ask for clarification.
  • Ask short questions that require short answers if needed.
  • Allow extra time without showing impatience.
  • Consider written communication as an alternative.

Don't

  • Finish sentences for the person.
  • Pretend to understand when you don't.
  • Rush the conversation.
  • Talk to someone else as if the person isn't there.

Cognitive & Learning Disabilities

Do

  • Offer directions or instructions both orally and in writing.
  • Read instructions aloud if asked.
  • Use plain language and avoid jargon.
  • Break complex information into smaller steps.
  • Allow extra processing time.
  • Provide written summaries of important conversations.
  • Be patient if information needs to be repeated.

Don't

  • Assume the person can't understand complex topics.
  • Use condescending language or tone.
  • Rush through explanations.
  • Make assumptions about intelligence.

Psychiatric Impairments

Do

  • Provide information in clear, calm, respectful tones.
  • Allow opportunities for questions.
  • Be patient and flexible.
  • Respect confidentiality.
  • Focus on the person's abilities, not limitations.
  • Offer breaks during long meetings if helpful.

Don't

  • Make assumptions about behavior or capabilities.
  • Use stigmatizing language ("crazy," "insane").
  • Discuss the person's condition with others.
  • Take unpredictable behavior personally.

Tips for Presenting & Teaching

When delivering presentations, hosting events, or teaching classes:

Before the Event

  • Ask about accommodation needs in registration or syllabus.
  • Ensure the venue is physically accessible.
  • Arrange for interpreters, captioners, or assistive listening devices if needed.
  • Provide materials in advance when possible.

During the Presentation

  • Use a microphone, even if you "project well."
  • Face the audience when speaking.
  • Verbally describe visual content on slides or demonstrations.
  • Repeat audience questions before answering.
  • Allow extra time for questions and processing.
  • Take breaks during long sessions.

Materials

  • Ensure slides have sufficient contrast and large text.
  • Provide alt text for images in handouts.
  • Offer materials in multiple formats (print, digital, audio).
  • Caption all videos.

Common Access Solutions by Disability

The following examples illustrate typical accommodations, though individual needs vary. Always discuss specific needs with the person.

Low Vision
  • Seating near the front of class or meeting room
  • Large print handouts, signs, and equipment labels
  • Monitor connected to microscope or lab equipment to enlarge images
  • Materials available in accessible electronic format
  • Screen magnification software
Blindness
  • Course materials in braille or accessible electronic format
  • Verbal descriptions of visual aids, charts, graphs, images
  • Raised-line drawings and tactile models of graphic materials
  • Braille equipment labels, auditory lab warning signals
  • Adaptive lab equipment (talking thermometers, calculators, light probes)
  • Computer with optical character recognition, screen reader, braille display
Hearing Impairment
  • Sign language interpreter or real-time captioning (CART)
  • FM system or hearing loop
  • Note-taker
  • Written instructions, lab manuals, summaries
  • Use of email for discussions
  • Visual warning systems for emergencies
Learning Disability
  • Note-taker and/or recorded class sessions
  • Captioned films and videos
  • Extended time on exams, alternative testing arrangements
  • Visual, aural, and tactile instructional demonstrations
  • Computer with voice output, spellchecker, grammar checker
  • Reading software for digital texts
Mobility Impairment
  • Note-taker, lab assistant, group lab assignments
  • Classrooms, labs, and field trips in accessible locations
  • Adjustable tables, lab equipment within reach
  • Materials available in electronic format
  • Computer with alternative input device (voice, alternative keyboard)
Health Impairment
  • Note-takers for missed classes
  • Flexible attendance policies when possible
  • Extended time on exams
  • Materials in electronic format
  • Email communication for updates during absences
