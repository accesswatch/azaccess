Course Description

This course introduces students to digital accessibility principles, guidelines (WCAG 2.2), and practical implementation strategies. Students will learn to evaluate and remediate digital content including websites, documents, and multimedia.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

  1. Explain the legal and ethical foundations of digital accessibility
  2. Apply WCAG 2.2 success criteria to evaluate digital content
  3. Remediate common accessibility barriers in documents and web pages
  4. Use assistive technologies to test digital content
  5. Advocate for inclusive design in professional settings

Best Practices for Accessible Syllabi

These guidelines are adapted from UW DO-IT's "Equal Access: Universal Design of Your Syllabus" by Dr. Sheryl Burgstahler.

Applying Universal Design to Your Syllabus

  • Equitable use (UD 1): Include information useful to students with diverse abilities, backgrounds, and technology skills.
  • Simple and intuitive (UD 3): Arrange information logically, use consistent formatting, keep language plain.
  • Perceptible information (UD 4): Ensure adequate contrast between text and background.
  • Multiple means of engagement (UDL 1): Explain relevance of content, integrate choices within the course.
  • Multiple means of representation (UDL 2): Provide the syllabus in print and accessible digital format; consider a video overview.
  • Multiple means of action (UDL 3): Describe multiple options students will have to demonstrate learning.
  • Accessible technology (WCAG): Use text-based formats, structure headings and lists, provide alt text for images.

Tips for an Accessibly Designed Syllabus

  • Use first person, friendly language. Make students feel welcome from the start.
  • Use plain language. Spell out acronyms, define terms, avoid unnecessary jargon.
  • Make expectations clear. Provide grading criteria, rubrics, step-by-step guidelines for assignments.
  • Use heading structure: Help students navigate with H2, H3 tags for screen readers.
  • Include accommodation information prominently: Don't bury it at the end.
  • Provide contact information in multiple formats: Email, phone, office hours, virtual options.
  • Describe building accessibility: Entrance locations, elevator access.
  • Use tables appropriately: Only for tabular data with proper headers.
  • Link to resources: DRC, tutoring, writing center with descriptive link text.
  • Offer materials in multiple formats: Note availability of audio, large print, digital.
  • Be specific about due dates: Include day of week and time zone.
  • Include campus resources: Tutoring centers, counseling services, disability services.
  • State behavioral expectations and communication preferences.
  • Avoid making your syllabus a PDF unless you ensure it's fully accessible. Use LMS pages or accessible Word documents instead.
